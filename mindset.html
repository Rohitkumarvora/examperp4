Key principles to understand and answer questions effectively.

Baby Step / Do Next

This mindset applies to questions asking "what should the project manager do next". The correct answer is the initial, logical action to address the problem, not the final solution.

Do Not Trouble Sponsor / Escalate

A PM should manage problems independently. Escalating to the sponsor is a last resort for issues beyond the PM's authority, like major strategic decisions.

Analysis Over Action

When faced with a problem, first analyze the situation, gather information, and understand the root cause before jumping to a solution. Make informed decisions.

Don't Shy Away from Responsibilities

A PM should not delegate their core responsibilities. However, seeking expert advice (e.g., legal, regulatory) is not shirking responsibility, it's being resourceful.

Avoid Extreme Answer Choices

Options proposing drastic actions (e.g., replacing the whole team, pausing the project for minor issues) are usually incorrect. The solution should be proportionate to the problem.

Simon Sinek's Golden Circle

Prioritize answers based on this model:

  • Why: Most preferred (purpose/reason).
  • How: Next preference (strategy/method).
  • What: Least preferred (specific actions).

Avoid the "Good Boy" Approach

Don't blindly accept requests to please stakeholders. Decisions must be based on data, analysis, and established processes, not on avoiding conflict.

Do Not Assume

Select options based only on information explicitly provided in the question. Avoid answers that rely on unstated assumptions or conditions.

Retain, Don't Replace

The PMI mindset emphasizes retaining and developing team members/vendors. Removal is a last resort for severe ethical or legal violations, not performance issues.

PM is Not a Postman/Clerk

The PM's role is to add strategic value, lead, and solve problems, not just perform clerical tasks like documenting or sending notifications without deeper analysis.

Emotional Intelligence / Empathy

Acknowledge and support team members' emotional needs. Demonstrating empathy is crucial for team morale, even if you can't solve the problem directly.

The Show Must Go On

Strive to keep the project moving. Pausing or stopping is a rare and extreme measure for critical issues only (e.g., safety, legal breaches).

Preventive Mindset

For "how could this have been avoided" questions, focus on proactive measures that could have been taken earlier in the project to prevent the issue.

Avoid Overloading Resources

Consider resource capacity and competency when managing workload. Don't indiscriminately reassign tasks without proper planning and consideration.

Coach / Educate

PMs must coach and educate team members, stakeholders, and sponsors on processes and concepts, especially when there is a lack of understanding.

PMI Code of Ethics (Gifts)

Politely decline any gifts from clients or customers. Accepting gifts can compromise professional ethics and create perceived biases.

Prioritization for Business Value

When the goal is delivering the highest business value, prioritization of work is key to ensure the most impactful items are delivered first.

Conflict is Normal

Conflicts are normal. The "collaborate/problem-solve" approach is the most effective technique, focusing on finding mutually acceptable solutions.

Strategic Communication

Communication should be strategic: deliver the right information to the right people at the right time. Avoid sharing everything with everyone.

Definition of Done vs. Ready

Ready: Criteria that must be met before a task can start.
Done: Criteria that must be met for a task to be considered complete.

Unlearn Mindset

Let go of real-world practices that may contradict PMI's principles. Adopting the "PMI way" of thinking is crucial for the exam.

Avoid "Wait and Watch"

This approach is generally not recommended. Proactive analysis and action are preferred over waiting for a problem to resolve itself.
